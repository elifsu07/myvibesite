Alfred Adler's Thought Structure and Individual Psychology



The relationship between Alfred Adler and Freud


Alfred Adler was one of the most distinguished of the young physicians who gathered at Freud's house in the early twentieth century to discuss the findings of psychoanalysis. However, he was the first to break away from this group.
Many who opposed Freud's pessimistic view of man as a destructive being, sincerely embraced Adler's description of man as a being capable of adapting to different situations, achieving great success and making creative and constructive efforts for the development of humanity.
Freud's monograph on dreams, published in the newspaper, was severely criticised. Adler, who wrote a review that sincerely defended Freud's views, thus attracted Freud's attention. Freud invited him to join the psychoanalytic group and a close friendship was established between the two physicians.
Freud sent some of his patients to Adler and appointed him as his personal physician. During this period Adler made important contributions to Freud. However, important differences of opinion gradually emerged between Adler and Freud. Finally, the Psychoanalytic Association decided to organise a series of meetings to listen to and discuss Adler's views. At the third meeting, Adler and Freud realised that their views were completely opposed to each other. Upon this event, Adler and 13 members left the association and Adler collected his views under the title of Individual Psychology.



Adler's Opinion

Adler believed that human behaviour could be explained by the analysis of one's own inner experiences.

He said that behaviours are determined by the future goals of people, and the concept of future is the thoughts and expectations for the future that exist at the moment. (fictional goal,fictional finalism)

Adler was the first researcher to argue that the most distinctive feature of man is his tendency to realise and maintain his integrity and freedom. He argued that man tries to maintain a certain consistency in his behaviour, even though he may react inappropriately and deviantly from time to time.

Every person is born with a certain physical structure. However, people differ in terms of their physiological equipment. Adler does not find these differences very important except mental retardation. According to him, every human being can do everything necessary for himself/herself and what is important despite the differences is what the individual can do with his/her own equipment.

Freud and his followers explain stuttering as a psychological behavioural pattern arising from instinctive impulses coming from the id and seeking satisfaction in the mouth. According to Adler, such a functional defect only becomes significant when it becomes a focal point in an attempt to gain esteem or to compensate for feelings of deficiency.(organ deficiency) A child who is unconsciously afraid of going to school may vomit in the morning because his stomach is his weakest organ. Freud's followers interpreted such a phenomenon as a kind of defence mechanism.Adler, on the other hand, evaluates the same event as the reflection of a state of psychological tension in a weak organ.

I agree with Adler on this point. What is important is the reaction of the child to the situation rather than his/her physical weakness. For example, there are many national, talented athletes with physical disabilities. These people hold on to life, do not give up and succeed.
